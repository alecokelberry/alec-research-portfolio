Investigating Caffeine Intake Prior To Exercise

Spring 2023
Alec Michael Okelberry Utah Valley University Honors B.S. in Exercise Science Thesis

Exploring Pre-Exercise Ergogenics

For my Honors thesis at UVU, I researched caffeine—a widely used pre-workout supplement—to understand its physiological impact during intense physical activity. I designed a controlled crossover study to evaluate how low (2.5 mg/kg) and moderate (5 mg/kg) personalized caffeine doses, consumed half an hour before exertion, influence the body.

The primary objective was to observe changes not only in physical output but also in the body's physiological stress markers, paying close attention to the autonomic nervous system through heart-rate variability (HRV) and heart-rate complexity (HRC) data.

“Our findings indicate that personalized caffeine supplementation based on body mass does not negatively alter cardiac rhythm or HRC amidst exercise. However, the sample size of 12 individuals and demographic constraints mean these results should be applied cautiously.”

Research Implications

The study concluded that ingesting caffeine at these tailored dosages (2.5–5 mg/kg) shortly before a workout induces slight adjustments in respiratory patterns at sub-peak exertion. However, it does not drastically shift broader performance indicators like total oxygen consumption, peak power, or critical stress measurements (HRV/HRC) during maximal testing. Consequently, active individuals may utilize these typical supplementation amounts to potentially enhance their workouts without the fear of adversely affecting their autonomic balance.

Study Design & Methods

The research was structured as a randomized, double-blind, and placebo-controlled trial to guarantee that the observed physiological changes were directly linked to the specific, body-weight-adjusted caffeine amounts.

Subject Demographics

The study cohort consisted of 12 healthy, active adults ranging from 18 to 35 years old, all maintaining a BMI below 30.

Testing Protocol

Each subject completed three separate testing sessions in a randomized, blinded sequence. Thirty minutes prior to testing, they consumed a placebo beverage or a drink containing caffeine scaled strictly to their body weight (2.5 mg/kg or 5 mg/kg).

Data Collection

Subjects underwent a progressive, graded exercise assessment on a stationary, electromagnetically controlled cycling ergometer until they reached voluntary exhaustion. Data was captured and scrutinized using a Cosmed metabolic cart for respiratory analysis, Polar sensors for continuous HR/HRV tracking, and Kubios software for repeated-measures statistical evaluation.

Primary Discoveries

The collected data exhibited minor respiratory adaptations, yet demonstrated remarkable consistency in overall exercise performance and autonomic stress regulation regardless of the caffeine dose administered.

  1. Consistent Autonomic Function: The timing of HRV thresholds, complex heart rhythm patterns, and overall cardiac variability remained unaffected by the intervention.
  2. Respiratory Adaptations: We observed small, yet statistically relevant, modifications to breathing frequency and tidal volume specifically at the gas-exchange threshold (p < 0.05).
  3. Unchanged Performance Variables: Maximum power output, heart rate maximums, overall ventilation, and oxygen uptake showed no significant variance between the placebo and caffeinated trials.

Conference Presentation & Publication

In addition to serving as my honors thesis, this research was a highly collaborative effort within the UVU Applied Human Performance Lab. The findings were subsequently presented by my colleague and co-author, Spencer Hadfield, at the ACSM Southwest Chapter conference.

The abstract, titled "The Effects of Low and Moderate Caffeine Doses on Cardiorespiratory Responses During Graded Exercise," was published in the International Journal of Exercise Science: Conference Proceedings (Volume 15, Issue 4, Article 22).

Application for Athletes

When dosed accurately by body weight, caffeine does not appear to induce excessive cardiac strain or offset the autonomic nervous system during vigorous training. This suggests athletes can comfortably implement pre-workout caffeine strategies at 2.5–5 mg/kg to pursue performance gains without incurring hidden physiological penalties.
