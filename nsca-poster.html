Cerebral Oxygenation During Arm vs. Leg Cycling

Summer 2023
Alec Michael Okelberry Utah Valley University & University of Central Florida

Investigating Prefrontal Oxygenation

Project Contribution

While I didn't present this poster at the NSCA Conference myself, I served as a co-investigator under the mentorship of my professor. He had collected the oxygenation data during his time in Florida, and upon arriving at UVU, offered me the opportunity to take on this project. I led the data cleaning and analysis, earning co-authorship on the final study before my graduation in Spring 2023 to focus on my honors thesis.

Study Overview

This collaborative study between Utah Valley University and the University of Central Florida used near-infrared spectroscopy (NIRS) to measure real-time blood oxygenation in the prefrontal cortex—the brain area linked to decision-making and focus.

Participants performed arm-cranking or leg-cycling matched for total energy burn (~100 kcal) at two specific intensities: moderate (below gas-exchange threshold) and heavy (above threshold).

The brain's oxygen supply during exercise is critical for performance, cognition, and recovery. While leg cycling is well-studied, arm cranking (common in rehab, upper-body athletes, and cross-training) had limited data—especially when total caloric work is equalized across modalities and intensities.

Purpose & Overall Finding

To examine prefrontal cortex oxygenated hemoglobin ([oxy-Hb]) responses before, during, and after isocaloric arm- vs. leg-cycling at moderate and heavy intensities. Overall, cerebral oxygenation is essentially the same whether you train arms or legs when total work is matched.

Methodology

A comprehensive study design utilizing Near-infrared spectroscopy (NIRS) to non-invasively monitor mixed-mode aerobic exercise.

Participants

30 healthy young adults (16 women, 14 men; age 23 ± 4 y).

Threshold Testing & Intervention

Initial sessions to test Gas-exchange threshold (GET) for both arm and leg cycling. Followed by four randomized sessions: arm or leg cycling at moderate (GET) intensity matching 100 kcal.

NIRS Monitoring

Continuous NIRS monitoring of prefrontal [oxy-Hb] at rest, during exercise, and 5-min post-exercise recovery.

Results & Key Findings

Findings show remarkable uniformity in brain blood flow between modalities, with significant surges occurring in the immediate recovery window.

  1. Modality Independence: No significant difference in prefrontal oxygenation between arm and leg cycling when total calorific expenditure is matched.
  2. Post-Exercise Surge: A substantial (~45%) increase in cerebral oxygenation occurred during the immediate 5-minute post-exercise recovery period compared to baseline rest.
  3. Intensity Effects: There was a small but statistically significant interaction effect where heavy intensity bouts yielded slightly higher oxygenation in recovery (71%) compared to moderate (66%).

Practical Takeaways for Coaches

  • Choose arm or leg cardio based on your goals—brain oxygenation will be the same.
  • Expect a ~45% bump in prefrontal oxygenation for at least 5 minutes after finishing a cardio session.
  • Heavy-intensity work may give a tiny extra edge in post-exercise brain blood flow.
  • Useful for rehab professionals, tactical athletes, or anyone using upper-body ergometry.

Conclusion

Cerebral oxygenation is essentially the same whether you train arms or legs when total work is matched. Both moderate and heavy aerobic sessions produce a substantial and lasting boost in brain oxygenation during the immediate post-exercise period.
